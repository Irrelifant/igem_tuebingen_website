Project 2020

Heavy Metal Biosensing

Heavy Metal Pollution

Heavy metal ion pollution in the environment has raised significant concerns for soil, plants and animals. Naturally occurring heavy ions such as Manganese, Nickel, Copper, Chromium, and Zinc are crucial in trace amounts, but have a hazardous impact when found in excess deposition.
An increase in environmental heavy metal ions is currently promoted through various ways such as wind/soil erosion, chemical manufacturing industries, fertilizers, fossil fuels and natural ores. Marine life is immensely harmed by continuous discard of sewage, industrial and farming waste into oceans, causing bioaccumulation of heavy metals in animals further up the food chain, among them humans.
This poses a significant health problem, with an estimated 8 million people in emerging and developing countries dying from the effects of heavy metal poisoning in 2012. These numbers illustrate the need for safe disposal of heavy metals, the basis of which is their reliable identification.

Example: Manganese

In plants, excess Manganese (Mn2+) prevents the uptake of essential ions disturbing biochemical processes, while in the human body, Mn may cause Parkinson-like illnesses. This issue can be exacerbated by climate change. An increase in groundwater and soil temperature disbalances variables like Mn2+ and DOC (dissolved organic carbon) which has major impacts on drinking water quality through microbial contamination and Mn deposition in water distribution systems. This effect was observed in reservations surrounding Tübingen, making us aware of this issue.

Our Solution

As part of the iGEM-2020 competition, we decided to genetically modify an E. Coli strain so that it can act as a biosensor for heavy metals. We plan on using riboswitches for their detection and phytochelatins for absorption and clearance.
Riboswitches are regulatory sections in the mRNA (messenger RNA) and alter its translation upon binding to specific molecules, in our case a heavy-metal ion. The riboswitch is combined with a QPI (Quad-Part Inverter) and a fluorescent protein on a plasmid incorporated in E. coli. The QPI consists of a ribosome binding site, a coding region for a constitutively expressed repressor, a terminator and a repressor-specific promoter. When activated, the riboswitch prevents translation of the repressor and the promoter is released. Accordingly, the fluorescent protein is expressed and the detection of the target molecule is easily possible. Ideally, the intensity corresponds to the heavy metal concentration in the sample.
Figure 1: Schematic description of the heavy metal riboswitch sensor system. Left: target molecule is present, right: target molecule is absent.
While the first heavy metal ion we want to detect is Mn2+, the final idea is to create a system where multiple heavy metals can be detected at once by connecting different fluorescent colors to the riboswitches.
To enable the simultaneous clearance of water from heavy metals, improving the environmental situation, metal-binding phytochelatins could be added to the riboswitch construct to also be translated if the target ion is present.
As this outlook implies the in-field application and the release of the genetically engineered bacteria into the treatment plant system, a biosafety construct must be implemented prior to its use. Accordingly, we plan on utilizing an inducible kill-switch compliant with the biosafety values of the iGEM competition.

OUR PROJECT

As part of the iGEM-2020 competition, we decided to genetically modify an E. Coli strain so that it can act as a biosensor for heavy metals. We are working on inserting the reporter gene for a red fluorescent protein in E. Coli, which is expressed as soon as the bacteria are exposed to heavy metals. We use riboswitches as heavy metal binding sites that mediate the expression of the reporter gene. Riboswitches are RNA sections of the 5 ́-UTR which, depending on the binding of ligands, regulate the transcription of a gene or translation of a mrna. The experimental approaches are not yet particularly mature in connection with riboswitches that bind heavy metals, so we hope that our project will contribute to current research and provide a platform for further approaches.

WHY HEAVY METALS?

Not only in emerging and developing countries, but also in the industrialized countries, the consequences of human influence on the environment can be felt directly. This is evidenced by increased levels of organic pollutants and increased concentrations of heavy metals in the air and in water, some of which even exceed the permissible limit values [1]. This poses a significant health problem, with an estimated 8 million people in emerging and developing countries dying from the effects of heavy metal poisoning in 2012 [2]. These numbers illustrate the need for safe disposal of heavy metals, the basis of which is their reliable identification.

WHY BIOSENSORS?

The conventional analytical methods for the detection of heavy metals work very specifically and are very sensitive. However, the often highly complex devices required for this are very expensive to purchase and maintain and require experienced personnel in handling and evaluating the results. This severely limits the handling of these devices in developing countries. Biosensors provide an alternative approach. Basically, this is a sensor that is equipped with biological components. The main advantages of biosensors are that they are inexpensive to manufacture and much easier to use. A relatively high level of sensitivity has already been demonstrated with various biosensors [3]. These properties predestine biosensors for future use in structurally weak regions.
